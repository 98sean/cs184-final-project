Summary

Radiance by Greg Ward is a gold standard in physical-based rendering, renowned for its ability to simulate realistic lighting in architectural and interior scenes. One of its key innovations is the use of ambient global illumination (GI)—an efficient technique for simulating indirect diffuse light, which is crucial for effects like color blending and soft, non-black shadows. Our goal is to study and implement ambient GI in the CS184 pathtracer (HW3), to simulate diffuse interreflections in interior spaces.

Problem Description

One challenge of Monte Carlo path-tracing is its significant performance limitations in interior scenes, as it requires thousands of samples per pixel to achieve acceptable quality due to the complex indirect lighting caused by inter-surface reflections. This approcach results in multiple hours of rendering for moderately complex scenes, making iterative design workflows impractical.

This project addresses this computational bottleneck by implementing Greg Ward's ambient global illumination algorithm from Radiance, which replaces expensive Monte Carlo sampling with spatial caching of ambient lighting values. We aim to demonstrate whether this approach can provide the theoretical 5-20x performance improvement for interior rendering while maintaining visual quality.

Baseline Results

For the baseline results, we aim to reproduce the visual qualities illustrated in Figure 2.1, which demonstrate the expected appearance of ambient global illumination with caching. Our goal is to achieve smooth indirect lighting, diffuse color bleeding between surfaces, and softened shadow regions where ambient light plays a significant role. These features indicate the successful integration of indirect light bounces using hemisphere sampling. To further validate our implementation, we plan to render several simple interior scenes, using flat colors in place of textures due to limitations in the current framework. Additionally, we will use area lights exclusively throughout the project, without implementing IES profiles or any complex light source as seen in the second example image.

In addition, we want to visualize cache points directly within our renderer—not only to validate interpolation quality and sampling density but also to assist in debugging and refinement of our caching strategy. This visualization will help ensure that our ambient cache adapts appropriately to scene geometry and lighting variation.

Aspirational Plan

  • Basic texture support: Simple diffuse texture mapping only
  • Cache optimization: Gradient-based cache validation
  • Advanced interpolation: More sophisticated cache weighting

Questions we plan to answer:

  • Performance Analysis: How significant is the speedup when replacing Monte Carlo global illumination with ambient caching in interior scenes? Can we achieve the theoretical 10-100x improvement?
  • Quality Trade-offs: What are the visual differences between ambient cached lighting and Monte Carlo global illumination? Where does ambient caching excel or fall short?
  • Implementation Feasibility: How challenging is it to integrate Ward's ambient caching algorithm into an existing pathtracer architecture? What are the main implementation hurdles?
